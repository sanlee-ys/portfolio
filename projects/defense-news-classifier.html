Defense News Classifier

An LLM classifier, and the eval harness that kept me honest about it.

This labels defense-news text on three axes: category (procurement, operations, policy, technology, industry), operational domain (air, land, sea, cyber, space, multi), and region (indo-pacific, europe, middle-east, africa, americas, global). That part is a single model call. The eval harness is the interesting part, and it’s where data-driven decision making actually gets tested: more than once it caught me being wrong, and I went with what it showed instead of my own instinct.

What the eval caught

Two moments show why the eval mattered at all.

The prompt that read better and performed worse

I sharpened the system prompt to draw a cleaner line between procurement (a purchase, the buyer's view) and industry (a company's own business news). It read better to me. Then the numbers came in: category accuracy fell from 79.0% to 76.7%, and industry recall dropped from 0.217 to 0.100. The sharper rule gave the model an even cleaner excuse to dump borderline company stories into procurement. I reverted it.

The lesson: sounding better and performing better aren’t the same thing. My gut couldn’t tell the difference here; the numbers could.

The grounding that did not pay

I added BM25 lexical retrieval to ground each classification in similar documents, expecting a clear win. Measured lift: +1.9% category accuracy (a net of one correct call in 54) and +0.0% on domain, where grounding fixed three calls and broke three. Lexical retrieval doesn’t earn the cost of moving to embeddings here.

Then it got worse for grounding, and the way I found out is the part worth telling. The comparison itself had a bug: I was scoring the new-prompt grounded arm against an old-prompt frozen baseline, which quietly inflated the lift. Rebuilding the baseline so both arms ran the same prompt killed the advantage outright. A three-pass confirm settled it: across 162 grounded classifications, grounding fixed a domain call zero times and broke four.

So I retired it (ADR-012): out of the shipped path, out of the CI gate. The retrieval code, the corpus, and the measurement scripts stay in the repo, dormant and reproducible, because the built-it-measured-it-cut-it trail is the artifact. The verdict hardened from “BM25 doesn’t justify upgrading to embeddings” to “BM25 doesn’t justify itself.”

The lesson: an unfair baseline flatters whatever you are hoping for. The first measurement said don’t bother; a fair one said cut it. Comparing two things means changing exactly one of them.

Why the numbers can be trusted

The eval has been rebuilt more than once, and the gaps between the versions are the honest part. v1 graded the model on 300 snippets it generated itself. Those numbers measure consistency, not correctness, and they hid a real hole: industry recall was 0.217, the model catching one in five. v2 replaced that with 54 hand-labeled real snippets (DoD news wire plus SEC filings), cross-checked by an Opus judge. On real financial text with actual vocabulary, industry went to an F1 of 1.000. A model graded on its own output only tells you it’s internally consistent. To know whether it’s actually correct, you need real data that a person labeled.

Domain rose to 94.4%; category didn’t move. I read that as a ceiling made of label ambiguity, which a stronger model can’t un-blur. I was half right, and the next column is where I found out.

The ceiling that wasn’t

Calling category a ceiling was a comfortable conclusion, so I went looking for the shape of the misses instead of accepting it. A per-cell confusion breakdown showed they weren’t scattered at all: they clustered on one boundary, technology read as operations whenever a new system was tested or demoed in an operational setting, plus a habit of over-assigning land. Two targeted rubric clauses moved category from 90.7% to 94.4% and domain from 90.7% to 92.6%, with technology recall reaching 1.000 and nothing regressing.

The lesson: “that’s the ceiling” is a hypothesis, not a finding, and it happens to be the one that excuses you from more work. It was a prompt problem wearing a ceiling’s clothes. Note what it wasn’t: a bigger model.

Two ways of not fooling myself

Fifty-four hand-labeled snippets is a small set, and a small set has a wide error bar. Two follow-ups exist to keep that honest.

Scaling the eval before trusting it

The 54-snippet gold set carries a 95% confidence interval about 13 points wide, which is wide enough that a couple of lucky rows look like progress. So I scaled to 300 real snippets graded by the Opus judge, which halves the interval: category 93.3% [89.9, 95.6], domain 90.3% [86.5, 93.2]. Those corroborate the hand-graded numbers rather than replacing them.

The caveat that stays attached: the judge is the answer key here, so this measures agreement with a model and inherits its roughly 5–6% disagreement with humans. The source wire is also operations-heavy, which makes the category macro-F1 uninformative — the report says so in the report, not in a footnote I can quietly drop.

The upgrade that bought nothing

The roadmap called for tiered routing: escalate the uncertain cases to the more expensive model. I built the trigger, measured it, and shipped the negative result. Routing moved +0 rows on both axes at about 1.97× the cost per article. Of nine escalations on the gold set it fixed zero and broke one, and even sending everything to the premium model scored the same 94.4% category, so there was no headroom for any router to capture.

Why it’s worth the build: the earlier prompt fix had already taken the ground routing was aimed at. The cheapest fix won, and now that’s measured rather than asserted. One more finding stayed in the record because it was inconvenient: adding a field to the schema flipped one benign snippet into a model refusal, so the measurement apparatus itself changed the behavior being measured. Full verdict in ADR-013. A separate project, measuring how reliable an LLM judge is, later landed on the same verdict from a different direction — three independent measurements now say escalation barely pays.

The third axis

v3 added region, which breaks the output contract on purpose: callers that expected two fields now get three. Six labels, with global as the single catch-all for stories with no geographic anchor and stories spanning several. That catch-all was the real design decision, and the alternative (splitting “no geography” from “worldwide”) would have put the fuzziest boundary on the most common kind of story in the corpus. The label set, the boundary rules, and the scope limits were written down before the build in ADR-014.

Region landed at 87.0% against fresh hand labels. The more interesting number is that the misses aren’t noise: all seven are stories whose true label is global that the model pulled to a specific region, usually americas. The rule says label the theater the activity happens in, and only when the text says so. The model keeps seeing a US actor and inferring a US theater, which is the no-guessing rule being hard rather than the model being sloppy. One named failure mode beats a scattered 13%, because a named one can be worked on.

Adding the axis cost about one category row (94.4% to 92.6%, inside the noise on a set this size) while domain held. That trade is stated rather than smoothed over. The scaled region eval is gated behind one measured condition, judge-versus-human agreement on region, which came in at 100.0% — so the gate is clear and the scaled run is earned, not assumed.

The decisions

Decision · ADR-002

Force structured output with tool-use, not prompt parsing

The model is called with a forced tool schema (all three fields required, strict enums), so an invalid label is rejected at the API layer before it reaches application code. When v3 added region, it was one more required strict enum — the schema is where a new axis goes.

Why: the schema becomes the contract, and the app code stays free of defensive parsing. Tradeoff: tool-use costs a few extra tokens and a more verbose call signature than plain chat. Out-of-enum responses were rare (once in 300) and a single re-sample handled them.

Decision

One model call, not a multi-step pipeline

Classification is a single call, not a generate-reasoning-then-classify chain.

Why: the goal was to measure classifier quality honestly, and a simpler baseline makes the eval cleaner to reason about. Tradeoff: gives up any lift a reasoning step might add, which the eval can always be used to revisit later.

Decision · ADR-004

Implement the metrics by hand

Precision, recall, F1, and confusion matrices are computed directly with pandas and arithmetic, no ML framework.

Why: the call here was to hold off on ML tooling until it earns its place, not to default to it because it’s there. It keeps the dependency surface to anthropic plus pandas. Tradeoff: about 40 lines that sklearn.metrics would replace, accepted for clarity.

Decision · SYS-005 (system-level)

In-process, idempotent writeback to the notes API

As a component of the larger system, the classifier is invoked by a FastAPI BackgroundTask in notes-api and writes labels back as namespaced tags with replace semantics, so reprocessing the same note converges instead of piling up duplicate tags.

Why: replace semantics make reprocessing safe by design, so idempotency is a requirement, not a nicety. Tradeoff: the background task carries real retry and error-handling logic. See The System for the full loop.

The ladder it’s climbing

This classifier is also the protagonist of the portfolio’s roadmap: an autonomy ladder. Each level is the same system handed more self-direction, not a separate demo. The axis is who drives — reaching for a tool doesn’t move you up a level; deciding for yourself does.

L1 a human runs it → L2 + a tool → L3 it decides it’s done → L4 agents coordinate

  1. L1 Single call Shipped · v1

    Prompt, structured output, the eval harness.

    Who drives A human runs each item.

  2. L2 Augmented Shipped v2.0.0, then retired

    BM25 retrieval grounds the label in a corpus.

    Who drives A human runs it; the model reaches for a tool.

  3. L3 Autonomous loop Half climbed

    Wrapped in a loop that iterates to an explicit done-signal.

    Who drives The system decides when it’s done.

    • rung 1 — prompt-optimization loop shipped, rode the v2.1.0 tag
    • rung 2 — agent-driven ML loop spec’d, unbuilt

  4. L4 Multi-agent To spec

    Decomposed: triage, classify, and a critic that can hand work backward.

    Who drives Multiple agents coordinate.

The L2 state is the honest one: the retrieval layer shipped in v2, was measured, and was then cut when a fair baseline showed it hurting (that’s “the grounding that did not pay” above). A rung that was climbed and then climbed back down is still part of the record, which is why it isn’t quietly relabeled. L4 has an honesty test of its own: a critic that can bounce a label backward for reclassification, not a linear pipeline with extra steps. That backward edge is where the metric-gaming story will get tested.

The unbuilt levels are written down before they’re built. ADR-006 records the decision to make the ladder the spine, and the living roadmap holds the per-level detail — so as each level lands, what shipped can be checked against what was promised. L3’s first rung is the prompt-optimization loop; its run replay is a real measured run, and the honest thing it shows is the prompt gaining on the set it was tuned against while slipping on held-out gold.

Stack

Python, the Anthropic SDK (Sonnet 5 for classification, Opus as the grading judge), pandas for the eval tables, FastAPI for the HTTP endpoint, and rank-bm25 for the retired grounding experiment. Tested with pytest on mocked calls. The repo is public: github.com/sanlee-ys/defense-news-classifier — the eval harness, gold set, and ADRs are all checkable, including the experiments that were measured and cut.

What it demonstrates

The dataset is small and the scale is personal. The transferable part is the method: evals work like unit tests for an LLM system — you find out before it ships, not after.
